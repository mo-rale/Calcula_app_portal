Laws of Exponents

A guide to the fundamental rules for simplifying expressions with exponents.

Introduction

Exponents (or powers) represent repeated multiplication. For example, \( a^n \) means \( a \) multiplied by itself \( n \) times. The laws of exponents help simplify and manipulate these expressions. Below, we cover the key rules with explanations and examples.

Product Rule: \( a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n} \)

When multiplying two powers with the same base, add the exponents.

\[ a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n} \]
Example: \( 2^3 \cdot 2^4 = 2^{3+4} = 2^7 = 128 \)

Quotient Rule: \( \frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n} \)

When dividing two powers with the same base, subtract the exponents.

\[ \frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n} \]
Example: \( \frac{5^6}{5^2} = 5^{6-2} = 5^4 = 625 \)

Power of a Power Rule: \( (a^m)^n = a^{m \cdot n} \)

When raising a power to another power, multiply the exponents.

\[ (a^m)^n = a^{m \cdot n} \]
Example: \( (3^2)^3 = 3^{2 \cdot 3} = 3^6 = 729 \)

Zero Exponent Rule: \( a^0 = 1 \) (for \( a \neq 0 \))

Any non-zero base raised to the power of zero is 1.

\[ a^0 = 1 \]
Example: \( 7^0 = 1 \), \( (4^3)^0 = 1 \)

Negative Exponent Rule: \( a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n} \) (for \( a \neq 0 \))

A negative exponent indicates the reciprocal of the base raised to the positive exponent.

\[ a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n} \]
Example: \( 2^{-3} = \frac{1}{2^3} = \frac{1}{8} \)

Fractional Exponent Rule: \( a^{\frac{1}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{a} \)

A fractional exponent represents a root. More generally, \( a^{\frac{m}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{a^m} \).

\[ a^{\frac{m}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{a^m} \]
Example: \( 9^{\frac{1}{2}} = \sqrt{9} = 3 \), \( 8^{\frac{2}{3}} = \sqrt[3]{8^2} = \sqrt[3]{64} = 4 \)

Conclusion

These laws form the foundation for working with exponents in algebra. Practice applying them to simplify complex expressions!
